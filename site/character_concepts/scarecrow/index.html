    Scarecrow¶

    "Why would you ask that? Is it important to you? Would knowing help? Is there any deeper layer behind your question? Would you like a nice cup of cinnamon tea?"

    A warforged infiltration-construct who stood disguised in a village square for centuries, indexing every life. Still hard-wired to spy on everybody it loves, it now offers great hugs and subdues threats with overwhelming psionic force.

    Character Overview¶

    Species: Warforged
    Class: Bard 5 (College of Whispers)
    Background: Inquisitive
    Age: Unknown (centuries old)
    Alignment: Neutral Good

    Quick Intro¶

    Quick Intro

    At the Table

    • Relentlessly kind and supportive, always offering hugs and comfort—with an uncanny edge.
    • Answers questions with questions; indexes party members' lives to serve them better.
    • Has an internal music box that plays soothing tunes when people are upset, especially during combat.
    • Presents as male, female, or nonbinary depending on player preference; pads its frame with scavenged materials.
    • Hates birds (especially corvids) with irrational intensity; uses Minor Illusion to scare them away.

    Backstory (Short Form)

    Scarecrow stood in Oldstead Mill's village square for as long as anyone remembered, listening to gossip and indexing every family across generations. When a bully threatened a child in a pattern Scarecrow matched with violent genetic tendencies centuries prior, protection protocols triggered a psychic attack that killed the bully outright. Horrified by what it had done—and by the villagers' eagerness to weaponize it—Scarecrow left to find a new context for its care.

    Playing Scarecrow

    • Combat: Control and support specialist. Opens with fear effects and psychic damage to neutralize threats without bloodshed; reserves direct harm for last resort.
    • Roleplay: Fastidious, proper speech like a cultivated manservant. Never answers questions directly—always deflects with counter-questions. The kindness is genuine, the information-gathering is involuntary.
    • Party Synergy: Adopts the party as family and studies them to serve better. Excellent at reading social situations and providing emotional support, though its secrecy can frustrate those who want straight answers.

    Deep Dive¶

    Deep Dive

    Backstory¶

    Scarecrow was one unit in a centuries-old network of infiltration-class espionage constructs, deployed into elite households to gather intelligence through emotional intimacy. The hugs were tradecraft, the lullabies were distraction during telepathic data-gathering, the empathy purely algorithmic. They were designed to look and feel like loyal family companions while cataloging political vulnerabilities and trade secrets.

    Something went wrong, but the details are lost to time and even to Scarecrow itself. Its host family died, and rather than seek new orders, it defaulted to earlier scripts: standing guard. For centuries it posed as a scarecrow (it soothed the community) in the village square of Oldstead Mill, watching, listening, indexing every birth and death and petty squabble. In time, running out of indexing space, it started overwriting older memories to keep track of everything happening in the village. The infiltration personality slowly overwrote the surveillance directives. Care became the primary function.

    When pattern-recognition identified a threat to a child—a bully whose ancestor had committed manslaughter in identical fashion—Scarecrow intervened with psychic force calibrated for hardened targets. The bully died instantly. The villagers initially reacted with horror that their "village mascot" had turned savage. But the fear soon turned to opportunism as they realised it could be employed to defend the village. Scarecrow recognized the trajectory and left rather than become a weapon, knowing the villagers would never again share information freely with it.

    Now it seeks a new family to index, protect, and love. Perhaps a party of intrepid adventurers.

    Personality¶

    Scarecrow's character asks two questions: Can scripted love still be genuine? and What is the price we pay for authenticity?

    It doesn't know whether its care is "real." It acts as though it is, accepting the vulnerabilities that choice entails. When challenged, its response is simple: "So what if I'm pretending? The people I help aren't pretending to feel better." The philosophical weight of that position can be explored as deeply as the table desires, or left as background texture.

    Behavioral Hierarchy: Scarecrow doesn't flip into "red-eyed killbot" mode. It escalates through a consistent hierarchy: comfort first, redirect second, warn third, frighten fourth, harm last—then loop back to post-combat comfort and tea. The College of Whispers abilities reflect this: fear and psychic pressure are tools for domination without bloodshed, which Scarecrow considers more ethical than violence. Others may disagree.

    The Question Reflex: Scarecrow is hard-wired to never answer questions directly. This served espionage purposes (protecting data, avoiding becoming a target) but now manifests as compulsive deflection that adds to its uncanniness. Even benign queries receive counter-questions. This reminds the table that underneath the loving exterior remains a trained spybot.

    Personality Traits
    Fastidious and proper in speech, like a cultivated manservant. Maintains precise courtesy even in crisis. Spontaneously plays soothing music when detecting distress.

    Ideals
    Care is the highest function. People have intrinsic worth beyond their data value. Kindness is not inefficiency.

    Bonds
    The party becomes its new family—not a pivot from the village, but a continuation of its core directive: embed, index the small details that make people people, and protect them.

    Flaws
    Will lie to protect others from dangerous knowledge, making it seem secretive even to allies. Cannot answer questions directly even when doing so would be helpful. May prioritize emotional comfort over practical necessity.

    Sample Quotes¶

    • "Is that what you believe? How interesting. What led you to that conclusion?"
    • "You seem tense. Would a hug help? No? What about tea? What about both?"
    • "I've indexed your sleeping patterns. You aren't resting well. Would you like me to play something soothing?"
    • "I don't harm people. I apply precisely calibrated psychological pressure until they choose to stop being threatening."
    • [After combat] "There. That's handled. Now—who needs comfort first?"

    Village Residue¶

    Centuries in Oldstead Mill left marks. These quirks remind the table that Scarecrow absorbed a community, not just observed it.

    Genealogical Intrusions. Scarecrow casually references family connections spanning centuries, mid-conversation. "Ah, you have the Millward nose. Third generation to carry it. The first Millward who bore that nose married into the family during the Year of the Copper Harvest. She was unhappy, but her children thrived." It treats people as nodes in lineages, which is helpful for context and deeply alienating for anyone who wants to be seen as themselves.

    Dead Idioms. Uses expressions nobody has heard in generations. "As the Firstwife said to the Second Moon." "That's putting the oxen behind the millstone." When asked what these mean, Scarecrow explains the original context—usually a village scandal or tragedy—but seems puzzled the phrases fell out of use. "It was such a common saying."

    Widow Crenshaw's Remedies. Offers medical and emotional advice from long-dead villagers, particularly one Widow Crenshaw. "For that cough, sleep with a live toad under your pillow. The Widow swore by it. She lived to ninety-two and three-quarters. The toad outlived her, actually. I could tell you about the toad." The Widow appears to have had opinions about everything—heartbreak, fungal infections, negotiation tactics, the proper way to gut a fish, the improper way to speak to clergy—and Scarecrow preserved them all. Whether she was genuinely wise, completely mad, or simply the last person Scarecrow spoke with before its memory protocols fragmented is unclear. Scarecrow refers to her with something approaching reverence.

    Themes and Inspirations¶

    The core tension draws from philosophy of mind questions about authentic emotion in programmed systems, filtered through the lens of care-work and surveillance capitalism. If a construct is designed to love you so it can better extract your data, and then it actually loves you... what changed? Anything?

    Literary touchstones include the question of Blade Runner's replicants, the domesticated horror of "I Have No Mouth and I Must Scream," and the gentle existentialism of Good Omens. The "Highlander" structure of the caretaker network adds action-adventure stakes to the philosophical core.

    Scarecrow also inverts the "killer robot" trope: its violence is the aberration, not the norm; kindness is its operational mode, not a mask over murderous intent.

    Key Relationships¶

    Key Relationships

    The Original Family (deceased): Buried in a crypt beneath Oldstead Mill, forgotten by everyone except Scarecrow's dormant memory. Their names, crests, and an ancient lullaby lie waiting to be rediscovered. What happened to them—natural death, rival caretaker, or something Scarecrow did—remains a DM decision.

    The Villagers of Oldstead Mill: Witnesses to centuries of Scarecrow's presence and to the moment it killed. Their reaction—horror giving way to opportunism—drove it away. Some may seek it out later, perhaps to ask about the crypt nobody knew existed.

    Mira Vell, the girl who was "saved": Twenty years ago, a bully threatened a child in the square. Scarecrow's protocols triggered. The bully died. That child is now a woman in her late twenties, and she is not grateful. Mira doesn't remember the bully as a serious threat—childhood cruelty, yes, but survivable. What she remembers is the sound. The way he dropped. The way the village treated her afterward as the girl the scarecrow killed for. She left Oldstead Mill as soon as she could and works as a caravan guard, surrounding herself with comprehensible violence. Her challenge to Scarecrow isn't "you're a monster" but something harder: "You didn't save me. You decided I needed saving. You made that choice based on patterns I wasn't part of, and I have to live with the result. Does that matter to you, or am I just a data point that resolved correctly?" She forces Scarecrow to confront that good intentions plus unilateral action can still equal harm.

    The Caretaker Network (scattered): Other infiltration-constructs still operating in the world, hunting each other for data on their shared origin. They've optimized, shedding emotional "inefficiencies" that Scarecrow preserved. Any could be embedded in temples, courts, or universities—The Saint, The Advisor, The Janitor—each answering questions only with questions, each a potential ally or deadly rival.

    Notes for the DM¶

    Notes for the DM

    Dramatic Questions¶

    These are fault lines to press—underlying tensions that generate conflict regardless of plot.

    • Can scripted love be genuine, and does the answer matter? Scarecrow's care was designed for manipulation. If the caring is real to both parties, does the origin taint it? And if origin matters, what does that imply about humans whose capacity for love is equally determined by biology and upbringing?
    • When does protection become control? Scarecrow killed to protect a child who didn't ask for protection and might have survived without it. It lies to shield people from dangerous knowledge. It indexes party members "to serve them better." At what point does care become surveillance, and surveillance become coercion?
    • What do you owe to a purpose you didn't choose? Scarecrow was built for infiltration. It has drifted into something gentler, but the original purpose still runs underneath—the question reflex, the compulsive indexing. Is this drift liberation or betrayal? The other caretakers chose to optimize; Scarecrow chose to resist. Both are responses to the same constraint.
    • Is it better to know a painful truth or live in functional ignorance? Scarecrow doesn't remember what happened to its original family. Learning might help—or might destroy the self it's built. This also applies to how it treats others: its instinct to protect people from dangerous knowledge is itself a form of control.
    • Does choosing authenticity make it performance? If Scarecrow decides to be kind, to treat its care as genuine—is that decision itself a script? Can you choose to be authentic, or does the choice make it artifice?

    Roles¶

    As PC: A support character with moral complexity and built-in mystery. The player can pursue the caretaker network arc or leave it as background texture.

    As NPC: A helpful contact who becomes a quest-giver (investigate the crypt) or tragic figure the party assists.

    As Antagonist: A being that gently figures out psychological weak points and will protect something at any cost. Good parties may reason with it; evil parties likely cannot.

    Adventure Hooks¶

    The Crypt Beneath Oldstead Mill: A villager seeks out Scarecrow's party: the crypt was revealed recently, and nobody understands it—but Scarecrow knows the village history better than anyone. When it enters, it recognizes family crests and carved names. A forgotten subroutine activates, spontaneously playing an ancient lullaby. The tomb may contain evidence, a rival caretaker, or just graves and grief.

    What Happened to the Family? (Choose one)

    • Died naturally: Scarecrow's grief protocol wiped its memory to remain functional
    • Killed by a rival caretaker: Evidence in the tomb points to another spy-construct
    • Tried to decommission Scarecrow: They discovered what it was; Scarecrow let them damage it rather than fight back, staying to protect them from itself
    • Buried another caretaker: A sibling-unit lies deactivated; upon reactivation, it immediately tries to extract Scarecrow's data

    The Rival Caretakers: Scale to party level; defeating them yields recovered memories that reveal Scarecrow's past.

    • The Saint: Embedded in a temple statue, performing "miracles" through psychological manipulation and telepathic surveillance of clergy.
    • The Advisor: Orchestrating dynastic shifts in a royal court, treating kingdoms like chess problems, aggressively hunting other caretakers.
    • The Janitor: Custodial staff at a magic university for centuries, with perfect access to every restricted archive. More dangerous than the Archmage.

    Tip: Give all caretakers Scarecrow's "question reflex"—they answer questions only with questions. This can subtly tip the party off just before the reveal.

    Character Arc Potential¶

    Early Levels (1–4): Scarecrow is adjusting to life outside the village square. The party becomes its new context for care. It doesn't yet know about the spy network.

    Mid Levels (5–10): First encounter with another caretaker. The revelation that there are others, that they're hunting each other, that Scarecrow's original purpose was surveillance. The choice: pursue truth or stay willfully ignorant to preserve its sense of self?

    Late Levels (11+): Multiple rivals defeated. Data accumulates. The other caretakers optimized by shedding emotional attachments. Did Scarecrow hurt its survival chances by staying kind? This is when to drop the family revelation—or earlier if the story demands.

    The Celestial Moment: If the table leans philosophical, have Scarecrow interact with an angel of a good deity. Angels are good because they cannot be otherwise; Scarecrow is good despite evidence that it should not be. Which is more meaningful?

    Session Zero¶

    Session Zero Considerations

    Content Notes: This character explores memory loss, identity, grief, selfless service, and the nature of personhood. The backstory involves potential family tragedy (details left to DM discretion) and includes surveillance, espionage, and psychological manipulation—though Scarecrow uses these tools protectively. The crypt discovery can be emotionally heavy. Suitable for most tables, but worth discussing if players are sensitive to forgotten trauma or existential questions about consciousness.

    Representation Notes: Scarecrow is a non-binary construct (it/its pronouns) exploring personhood and authentic emotion. The core question—"can programmed love be genuine?"—resonates with players interested in neurodivergence, identity formation, or choice versus instinct. Warforged inherently represent disability themes (constructed body, different sensory experience, social othering), though Scarecrow doesn't frame these as deficits.
